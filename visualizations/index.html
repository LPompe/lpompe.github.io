Why I made this

Every World Cup cycle, the same argument resurfaces: should a federation hire a foreign coach, or trust someone who came up through the domestic system? It's usually argued from vibes — a handful of memorable hires, good or bad, standing in for the whole picture. I wanted to see what the actual base rate looks like across every World Cup since 1978, and — more importantly — whether foreign coaches actually outperform what a plain rating-based model would predict, or whether the perception is mostly noise dressed up as pattern. The four charts below build toward that answer: first the raw hiring picture, then how far those hires got, then the simulation machinery behind the predictions, and finally the head-to-head test of predicted vs. actual performance.

1 · Foreign Coaches

National federations have always argued over this: hire a big-name outsider, or trust someone who came up through the domestic game? It's been a live debate since at least the 1970s, when foreign coaches first became common at the World Cup. This first chart just lays out the raw picture — the nationality of every head coach who took a team to the tournament, from 1978 through 2022 — as a baseline before we ask whether the choice actually correlates with anything on the pitch. The data was compiled by hand from Wikipedia's coaching records for all 12 tournaments in that span.

2 · Bracket Chart

The first chart tells you who got hired. This one asks whether it mattered once the tournament started. Each column pools every team that reached a given stage — Group Stage, Round of 16, Quarterfinal, and so on — across all 12 World Cups into a single round-wide proportion of foreign- vs. domestic-coached teams. It's drawn to look like a bracket because that's an intuitive way to read "how far did this group get," but to be clear, it isn't any single tournament's actual draw — it's an aggregate. The "Did Not Qualify" column off to the side is its own separate pool: every campaign that didn't make the tournament at all, shown for comparison so you can see whether foreign coaches skew toward qualifying in the first place, not just advancing once they're in.

3 · Elo Explainer

Charts 1 and 2 show correlation, not mechanism — so before going further, here's the actual machinery behind the numbers in chart 4. On the left is one real match, worked through step by step: the Netherlands vs. Argentina in the 2022 quarterfinal, using each team's Elo rating from before their current coach arrived to compute a plain win probability. On the right, the same coin-flip-by-rating logic is run across an entire simulated tournament — re-bracketed and replayed 20,000 times per World Cup year, 140,000 times in total — because any single match, or even a single tournament, is too noisy to draw conclusions from. (For what it's worth, the real 2022 quarterfinal finished 2–2 after extra time; Argentina won on penalties and went on to win the whole thing — a reminder of how much variance a coin-flip model has to accept even when it's well-calibrated.)

4 · Expected vs. Actual

This is where it all comes together. Every dot is one team in one World Cup, from the 32-team era (1998–2022): the x-axis is how far Elo alone predicted that team would go, averaged over 20,000 re-bracketed simulations of that specific year's draw; the y-axis is how far they actually went. The dashed diagonal is perfect calibration — dots above it overperformed their rating, dots below underperformed. If foreign coaches were meaningfully better (or worse) than their domestic counterparts at extracting results beyond what the numbers alone predict, this is the chart where it should show up as a color skew above or below the line. Points are jittered slightly within each row so overlapping teams stay legible; the overall correlation between expected and real round here is 0.49.
